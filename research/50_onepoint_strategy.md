## One Paragraph (elevator)

OnePoint is the voice-first shared brain for non-medical caregiver teams and the owner-operators of the small assisted-living facilities they eventually hand their loved ones to — one product that turns a 90-second voice note into a FHIR-structured, legally defensible care log, cites peer-reviewed literature back at the caregiver who just spoke, and is the same record from the first prospect phone call through the day-365 shift note. It is different because nobody else ships the family-to-facility bridge: consumer caregiver apps have no placement pipeline and no legal audit, enterprise senior-living CRMs have no voice capture and no family-facing surface, and lead marketplaces profit from *not* placing people well. It is now because A Place for Mom is bleeding trust under a $6M TCPA settlement and a Senate probe (see `20_competitor_matrix.md §14`, `30_gap_analysis.md §Where Competitors Are Vulnerable`), the single-facility owner-operator is an orphaned segment the Aline/Yardi stack openly refuses to serve (*"it will quickly teach your clients why they need a different product"* — `21_review_intelligence.md §Cluster 7`), and the exact AI stack required to run it (LiveKit + Medplum + LangGraph + Europe PMC) only became OSS-commercial-safe in the last twelve months (see `11_oss_landscape.md §Executive Summary`).

## Why OnePoint Wins

The senior-care software market is structurally bifurcated and leaves a specific person stranded between the camps. On one side are free consumer caregiver apps (CaringBridge, Lotsa, Ianacare, CircleOf, CareTree) that are legally toothless, medically blind, and revenue-starved — they cannot fund the features their users actually need because their business model cannot. On the other side sit enterprise senior-living CRMs and EHRs (Aline, Yardi, Sherpa, Enquire, ECP, PointClickCare, MatrixCare) designed for 10+ community chains and priced through six-week demo cycles that make single-facility economics collapse. Between them sit referral marketplaces (A Place for Mom, Caring.com) whose business model is reselling families to the highest bidder, currently under federal investigation for deceptive practices and a $6M TCPA settlement (`30_gap_analysis.md §Thesis`, `20_competitor_matrix.md §14`). The orphaned user is Ali Ann — the non-medical professional companion running a small caregiver practice who is simultaneously trying to place a tenant into the owner-operated assisted-living facility she is associated with. Every product in the matrix solves ≤30% of her job.

The thesis holds because the five gaps in `30_gap_analysis.md` are each ranked 4/5 or 5/5 severity, each mapped to specific FRs, and each cannot be closed by any single incumbent without rebuilding its buyer profile. Voice-first capture means rewriting EHRs that charge per click-field. Low-tech family inclusion means a non-SPA surface that every engineer hire at a competitor pushes back toward React. Legal-grade audit for a non-medical companion means being HIPAA-capable for a non-licensed buyer — a product contradiction most vendors refuse to resolve. AI medical research against peer-reviewed literature means framing outputs to threaded the FDA SaMD needle (`31_ai_advantage.md §Defensibility`). And the unified placement-to-care-log bridge means merging a CRM and an EHR at the database, which no enterprise competitor will do for a single-operator market.

OnePoint wins technically because every gap maps to a specific OSS pick that only became commercial-safe recently: Medplum (Apache 2.0, HITRUST/SOC2/HIPAA) for the FHIR backbone, Krayin (MIT — the only actively-developed MIT kanban CRM; every alternative is AGPL) for the placement pipeline, LiveKit Agents (Apache 2.0) for voice+vision, Better-Auth (MIT) for magic-link low-tech auth, HTMX (BSD-2) for the sister-on-a-flip-phone surface (`11_oss_landscape.md §Executive Summary`). The entire v0 stack is one Docker Compose with seven OSS services behind a thin TypeScript gateway. Total custom code: a ~50-line SHA-256 hash chain over Medplum AuditEvent, the Medplum↔Krayin bridge, the HTMX pages, the spreadsheet-mirror sync, and the iMessage reader. Everything else is configuration (`40_system_architecture.md §Summary`).

OnePoint wins economically because the unit economics work today. The full AI loop costs ~$6.50/month/care-recipient on Claude Haiku 4.5 with Sonnet 4.6 escalation and Opus 4.6 reserved for compound-signal trend detection (`31_ai_advantage.md §Cost Model`). Hosting on a Hetzner CAX31 is $18/month. A Sherpa CRM seat is $525/month per community. A single A Place for Mom commission is ~$3,500. OnePoint is cheaper than one commercial eMAR seat from Yardi and cheaper than one APFM referral, and every dollar spent compounds into a FHIR data asset the operator owns. The margin headroom is dramatic.

OnePoint wins commercially because the wedge customer exists on Saturday. Ali Ann needs a care log for Connie this week, is currently defending against a threatened lawsuit whose logs are what's saving her today (`OnePoint_PRD.md §10`), and has an open-vacancy assisted-living facility in Longmont that needs a tenant immediately (`OnePoint_PRD.md §2, §5.11, §10`). Every other product in this market would need a sales cycle to reach that customer. Ali Ann is already on the phone with the builder.

First-conversation hook: *"Are you paying A Place for Mom a month's rent per move-in, and getting contacted by families after their parent has already died? We're the one-facility alternative."*

## The Two-Segment Play

Most products that try to serve two segments at once die — the segments dilute each other, the roadmap forks, the brand gets muddy. OnePoint is different because the two segments are not parallel; they are the **same record, observed at two points in its lifecycle**. The non-medical companion's care log for a loved one at home and the owner-operator's placement pipeline for a prospective resident are two faces of the same job-to-be-done: *keep track of one aging person across the transition from "being cared for at home" to "being cared for in a facility," without losing the thread.*

The compound arrives on move-in day. In `40_system_architecture.md §Data Flow: Prospect → Move-In → Care Log`, a Krayin CRM Lead creates a "shadow" Medplum FHIR Patient the moment fit-assessment passes — not on move-in day, but weeks earlier at the Qualified stage. Every tour note, pre-admission document, fit score, and family conversation captured during the placement process accrues to that shadow Patient as FHIR Communication and DocumentReference resources. When Krayin flips the stage to `Resident`, the Bridge runs a single transactional update: `Patient.active = true`, the shadow flips to an active care record, the Encounter starts, the CarePlan draft activates. **Zero re-entry because there was never a second system.** The family who was using the shareable HTMX link to read tour notes during placement continues using the same link to read shift logs during residency. The sister on the flip phone never logs out.

This is why the segments compound instead of dilute. (a) Every placement fills a facility — the owner-operator revenue side — *and* seeds a 14-entry care log and a warmed mem0 memory graph before the caregiver ever records a single voice note. That care log is the upsell into the per-care-recipient subscription. (b) Every care-logging customer generates referrals when their loved one needs placement; Ali Ann's non-medical companion persona *is* the referral channel into the facility, the category no enterprise CRM cultivates because its sales motion cannot convert it. (c) The AI loop — AI-01 voice-to-log, AI-03 literature research, AI-05 voice prospect intake, AI-08 move-in handoff (`31_ai_advantage.md §Feature Designs`) — composes into the "Zero Re-Entry Care Journey" that neither a consumer caregiver app nor an enterprise CRM can ship without rebuilding its schema. (d) Every facility placement adds compliance documents (CO-602, POLST, TB, physician attestation) to the same FHIR store that holds voice logs, which means the legal-defensibility story *compounds across segments*: the export that saves Ali Ann from a client lawsuit is architecturally identical to the export that proves regulatory compliance for the Longmont facility.

ECP is the only competitor that builds *any* bridge, and their bridge is operator-to-operator inside a licensed-facility stack; the family never sees the ECP care record, and ECP's buyer is a licensed nurse (`20_competitor_matrix.md §12, §The Bridge Gap`, `30_gap_analysis.md §Gap 5`). OnePoint's bridge runs family → facility → family, with the same shareable link surviving the handoff. That architectural difference is the whole thesis.

## The Three Moats

**Moat 1 — The Compound Loop.** The voice blob becomes five coordinated artifacts in one transaction (`31_ai_advantage.md §Executive Summary`): a FHIR Observation, a Communication narrative, a Timesheet row, a DiagnosticReport with cited literature, and a mem0 memory update — all inside a hash-chained FHIR record. A competitor who clones feature AI-01 still cannot run AI-03 without the FHIR schema, AI-07 without the memory graph, or AI-08 without the unified Krayin↔Medplum bridge. Cloning the full loop means rebuilding the stack. Over time the per-patient mem0 graph *is* the product — six months in, it carries the recipient's baseline gait, afternoon confusion patterns, medication history, and rejection memory. That graph is not portable to any competitor's text-column-beside-a-billing-row data model.

**Moat 2 — Trust Posture as a Legal Commitment.** OnePoint's "one family, one facility, no lead resale, self-hostable if we disappear" is a commitment that competitors with an existing business model cannot copy. A Place for Mom's referral-fee model is structurally misaligned with it (`30_gap_analysis.md §Anti-Goals`). Every enterprise CRM's partner-integration pipeline is a gravity well pulling them toward lead aggregation. Better-Auth magic-link + HTMX + Medplum self-host is not just a technical stack; it is a set of architectural choices that make the trust promise *literally unbreakable* — we cannot sell data we never centralized. Every attorney-requested legal export that gets accepted by counsel is a credentialing event. Every active-litigation case where OnePoint logs survive a subpoena compounds into the next caregiver's decision to migrate. This moat deepens with every courtroom, not with every marketing dollar.

**Moat 3 — Velocity Asymmetry via OSS + Solo-Builder Ship Cadence.** Aline has 100+ partner integrations; every new feature at Aline crosses three product teams and a compliance queue. Yardi's eMAR has documented 11-minute-per-resident load times because nobody owns latency (`21_review_intelligence.md §Cluster 3`). OnePoint ships the entire stack as one Docker Compose and one TypeScript gateway, with total custom code measured in low thousands of lines because every load-bearing system is already OSS (`40_system_architecture.md §Component Inventory`). A feature that takes Aline 6 quarters takes OnePoint 6 days. The most under-appreciated moat in enterprise software is that incumbents literally cannot move this fast — their organizations prohibit it. OnePoint's solo-builder cadence compounds every week the incumbent's roadmap doesn't ship.

## The Biggest Risks

**Risk 1 — Founder bandwidth collapse (likelihood: high, impact: high).** Ali Ann's dad relocation is an active constraint on *her* bandwidth, and Jero is a solo builder running a hop-3 operational agent host while building OnePoint. Any week that requires both full-day focuses will slip. **Mitigation:** the Week 1 cut is independently useful — record, save, share the link — even if weeks 2–4 slip by a month. The architecture explicitly degrades gracefully (`40_system_architecture.md §Risk Register`). Every week's deliverables are isolated enough that a two-week slip anywhere doesn't cascade. The Saturday working session is the single hard synchronous dependency; after that, the plan can breathe.

**Risk 2 — ECP pivots to family-facing before OnePoint reaches critical mass (likelihood: low-medium, impact: critical).** ECP is the one competitor that credibly builds any form of the move-in handoff (`20_competitor_matrix.md §12, §The Bridge Gap`). If ECP ships a family-facing HTMX surface and a voice-capture mode in the next 12 months, OnePoint's wedge narrows fast. **Mitigation:** ECP's buyer is a licensed facility administrator and their sales motion is ~50-state-compliance-heavy enterprise. Adding voice + a low-tech family surface + an AI research agent requires rebuilding ECP's buyer profile. We monitor ECP's release notes monthly; we move faster than they can reorganize. The real defense is the trust posture moat — even a cloned ECP cannot match "self-hostable, no lead resale, text the founder" without tearing up its own commercial model.

**Risk 3 — Regulatory exposure on the AI research agent (likelihood: low, impact: critical).** If the research agent is ever framed as diagnostic, OnePoint enters FDA Software as a Medical Device territory and the entire product becomes a compliance burden. If a family acts on a research finding and a patient is harmed, OnePoint is the proximate-cause defendant. **Mitigation:** FR-14 is enforced at the `frame_for_team` LangGraph node: every finding ends with "bring this to a licensed professional," and the evaluation harness has a red-team test that fails the build on any output framed as a diagnosis (`31_ai_advantage.md §Evaluation Plan`, `40_system_architecture.md §Risk Register`). The hard rule: zero treatment recommendations, only cited literature summaries. Product copy, UI framing, and the export PDF all reinforce this. Legal counsel reviews the research-agent framing before any customer beyond Ali Ann is onboarded.

**Risk 4 — The chosen stack has a load-bearing unknown (likelihood: medium, impact: medium).** Medplum self-host has operational gotchas under load; Better-Auth is young (27.8k stars, ~2 years old); Krayin is a PHP/Laravel project sitting inside an otherwise-TypeScript monoculture and its upgrade path could break custom pipeline stages; LiveKit server infra is a real operational burden. None of these have been stress-tested by OnePoint's specific workload (`40_system_architecture.md §Risk Register`). **Mitigation:** the Gateway abstracts every OSS dependency so any single swap is one component, not the whole app. Krayin is isolated behind the Bridge — Jero never edits Laravel core. LiveKit Cloud is a documented fallback if self-hosting eats too much time. Better-Auth can swap to Auth.js if it stalls. The v0 cut explicitly caps at 3 care recipients so load stays sub-critical through the validation phase.

**Risk 5 — The low-tech sister hates the HTMX surface on her first interaction (likelihood: medium, impact: high).** FR-7 is a hard architectural constraint, and the product assumes that an HTMX + server-rendered page works on a 2015 iPhone held by a 5G-skeptical relative. If the first interaction fails, adoption collapses before it starts — and adoption through the low-tech sister is what makes OnePoint different from every consumer caregiver app that excludes her. **Mitigation:** the Week 4 release bar explicitly includes "test on a real old Android browser before any family touches it; if it fails, it fails the release" (`40_system_architecture.md §V0 Cut Week 4`). Ali Ann's 5G-skeptical sister is the acceptance test, and the test is synchronous — no release to the family is cut without her touching it first.
